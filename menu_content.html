ü´Ä What is Heart Disease?

Heart disease, also known as cardiovascular disease, refers to a group of conditions that affect the structure and function of the heart. This includes:

  • Coronary artery disease (CAD): Narrowing or blockage of the blood vessels that supply the heart muscle.
  • Heart failure: A condition where the heart cannot pump blood effectively.
  • Arrhythmias: Abnormal heart rhythms, which can cause the heart to beat too fast, too slow, or irregularly.
  • Valve disease: Damage or malfunction in one or more of the heart‚Äôs valves, affecting blood flow.
  • Congenital heart defects: Heart problems that are present from birth.

‚ö†Ô∏è Causes of Heart Disease

  • Atherosclerosis: The buildup of fats, cholesterol, and plaque in artery walls, which restricts blood flow.
  • High blood pressure (hypertension): Forces the heart to work harder than normal, which can enlarge and weaken it.
  • High cholesterol: Can lead to plaque buildup in the arteries, reducing or blocking blood flow.
  • Diabetes: High blood sugar levels damage blood vessels and the nerves that control the heart.
  • Smoking and alcohol: Nicotine raises blood pressure and damages vessels; excessive alcohol can weaken the heart muscle.
  • Obesity and physical inactivity: Increase the risk of other conditions like diabetes, high cholesterol, and high blood pressure.
  • Unhealthy diet: Diets high in saturated fats, trans fats, sugar, and salt contribute to heart disease risk factors.
  • Stress and poor mental health: Chronic stress can damage arteries and worsen risk factors such as high blood pressure.
  • Genetics/family history: Having a family history of heart disease increases your risk, especially at a younger age.
  • Infections: Certain infections like rheumatic fever or viral myocarditis can cause inflammation and damage to the heart.

üîç Risk Factors

There are two types of risk factors:

1. Modifiable (you can change them):

  • Poor diet: Increases bad cholesterol and blood pressure, and leads to weight gain.
  • Lack of exercise: Weakens the heart muscle and contributes to obesity and diabetes.
  • Smoking: Narrows blood vessels, increases clot formation, and lowers oxygen supply to the heart.
  • High blood pressure: Makes the heart work harder and can lead to thickened arteries.
  • High cholesterol: Promotes plaque buildup and arterial blockages.
  • Diabetes: Increases the risk of coronary artery disease due to high blood sugar effects on arteries.
  • Obesity: Contributes to high cholesterol, diabetes, and high blood pressure ‚Äî all heart disease risks.
  • Stress: Long-term stress can increase blood pressure and lead to unhealthy coping habits.

2. Non-modifiable (you can‚Äôt change them):

  • Age: The risk of heart disease increases as you get older.
  • Gender: Men are generally at higher risk, though risk rises for women after menopause.
  • Family history: A history of heart disease in your family can increase your risk.
  • Ethnicity: Some groups have higher risk due to genetic and lifestyle factors (e.g., South Asians, African Americans).

‚ù§Ô∏è Symptoms of Heart Disease

The symptoms of heart disease can vary depending on the type, but common signs include:

  • Chest pain or discomfort (angina): Often feels like pressure, squeezing, or fullness in the chest.
  • Shortness of breath: May occur with exertion or even at rest due to poor heart function.
  • Fatigue or weakness: Especially during physical activity, as the heart fails to deliver enough oxygen.
  • Palpitations: A sensation of a racing or irregular heartbeat.
  • Swelling in legs or ankles: Caused by fluid buildup when the heart doesn‚Äôt pump effectively.
  • Dizziness or fainting: Can be a sign of arrhythmias or low blood pressure.
  • Pain in arms, neck, jaw, or back: Especially in women, heart-related pain may radiate to other parts of the body.

üõ°Ô∏è Prevention of Heart Disease

Heart disease can often be prevented by making simple lifestyle changes. Key preventive steps include:

  • Eat a heart-healthy diet: Focus on fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats. Limit sugar, salt, and processed foods.
  • Stay physically active: Aim for at least 30 minutes of moderate exercise (like walking, cycling, or swimming) most days of the week.
  • Quit smoking: Stopping smoking significantly lowers the risk of heart disease within just a few years.
  • Control blood pressure and cholesterol: Monitor regularly and take medications if prescribed by your doctor.
  • Manage diabetes: Keep blood sugar levels within the target range through diet, exercise, and medications if needed.
  • Maintain a healthy weight: Even small weight loss can reduce risk factors like high blood pressure and cholesterol.
  • Reduce stress: Try yoga, meditation, deep breathing, or talking to someone to manage chronic stress.
  • Limit alcohol: If you drink, do so in moderation (1 drink per day for women, 2 for men).
